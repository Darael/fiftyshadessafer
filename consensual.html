CONSENSUAL: The importance and meaning of consent

Consent is always a tricky issue, and becomes even trickier where kink is involved. But the most important thing to remember is that you should never do anything that you aren't comfortable with. Whether you're a submissive or a Dominant, it's vital to make sure that both you and your partner are happy and consenting.

Here are some important questions to ask yourself:

Do I want this?

Obviously the most important question in consent, this is also the simplest. Do you want to do this thing – not because the other person wants to, not because you think you ought to want to, but because you want to? If not, then you need to stop, let the other person know you're not comfortable, and either do something else or end entirely. If your partner will not stop a scene when you tell them to, this is rape. Even if they say they couldn't help themselves, or that you consented at the beginning of the scene, it is still rape.

Does the other person want this?

This can be harder to be certain of. Ask your partner whether they're okay – not just before a scene, but preferably throughout it as well. Make sure that they don't feel pressured, and that they're in a fit state to consent (i.e., not drunk, not under the influence, etc). Whether they're a Dominant or a submissive, you have a responsibility to your partner to make sure they're not doing something they don't want to. If they sound uncertain or apathetic about their consent, it's best to double-check – if you're not completely sure they want it, don't do it. You can always find something else to do which doesn't overstep their boundaries, even if it's vanilla sex or just cuddling and watching TV.

Does everyone involved understand what's being offered?

Before you start a scene, it's important to be absolutely sure that everyone involved knows what that scene will involve, the risks that might be included, and what their role in it will be. It's fun to be spontaneous and exciting, but it's also hazardous, so if something new gets introduced to a scene, it's important to make sure your partner understands your intentions and is okay with them.

Am I being pressured into consent?

This can be a hard question to ask yourself, but it's important. Is your partner making you feel as if you have to do something, making either implicit or explicit threats? Do you feel as if you have to agree to something in order to be a “good” submissive or Dominant? If you feel like not giving consent will lead to negative consequences (e.g., your lover will leave or be disappointed in you, you will have failed at kink, you will disappoint them, etc.), it is important to take a step back from the situation and reassess. If your partner threatens you with punishment for witholding consent, this is a MAJOR red flag for abuse. Any loving Dominant will accept it if you say that you don't want to do something, and will give you the space and time to figure out what's right for you.

Do I know how to revoke my consent?

Because of the nature of BDSM, it's quite normal to realise halfway through a scene (particularly one involving pain or sensation play) that you aren't as comfortable with it as you thought you would be. If this happens, it's important that you can let your partner know, and that they will stop. There are some scenes where just saying “no” or “stop” can be misconstrued, especially if you're roleplaying, so make sure that you have a safeword that you and your partner can remember under stress, and that you're not afraid to use it. If you plan on doing scenes involving oral sex or gagging, it's also a good idea to agree on a hand signal beforehand, so you can still safeword even when you can't speak.

If you answer all these questions with “yes”, then that's great! But if you're unsure about any of them, then it's a good idea to stop, take a step back, and discuss your concerns with your partner. Being unsure about a scene is always valid – you should never be made to feel ashamed or insecure about saying there's something you don't want to do, or asking for more information. It is not part of BDSM to ignore somebody's concerns about consent. If someone tells you that your relationship with them relies on you consenting to an act you're uncomfortable with, then that relationship isn't worth it.

Kink is fun, sexy, and exciting. Rape is none of those things. And if someone is forcing you into something sexual that you're not comfortable doing, that is rape.
