SANE: Danger signs

Because BDSM is built around power dynamics, it can often tip over into abusive behaviours. These are not a part of BDSM, and if you think the relationship is becoming abusive, it's best to find a way out. Here are some behaviours you should watch out for:

  • Pressuring you into acts you're not comfortable with. Whether it be getting into a BDSM relationship in the first place, a specific scene, or the way in which you live as a couple, consent is a vital part of any relationship. If you've made it clear you're uncomfortable with doing something, no matter how trivial, no responsible Dominant should try to force you into it.

  • Expecting your bedroom dynamic to count everywhere else. Unless you've specifically agreed to make BDSM a part of your general lifestyle, then being your Dominant in sexual situations does not give someone the right to control your life in other ways.

  • Acting entitled to your time/attention. This applies to sex, but to other things as well. If your Dominant expects to be the highest priority in your life, and expects you to drop everything to pay attention to them, this is a serious red flag. Again, it's possible for this to be part of your arrangement, but it has to be something discussed and consented to in advance.

  • Ignoring safewords or making you feel bad for using them. Safewords are a vital part of BDSM, and they have to be respected. If your Dominant continues regardless after you've used a safeword, this is rape. If your Dominant acts as if respecting your safeword is an inconvenience to them, or as if using your safeword is a sign of weakness, this is also extremely unhealthy. Your Dominant's attitudes to safewords can be taken as their attitudes to consent. If they consider it anything less than vital, you should be very wary.

  • Controlling your relationships with other people. Whether this takes the form of jealousy around your friends, or of telling you not to discuss the relationship, your partner should not have control over your other relationships. Obviously, they are allowed to have concerns, but if those concerns take the form of controlling behaviour, then that is a major danger sign. At the end of the day, your relationships are your business.

  • Ignoring hard or soft limits. Both hard and soft limits can change over time, but that change always has to come from the person whose limits they are. Don't accept “just this once”. Don't accept “we'll build up to it”. Somebody who doesn't respect your limits is somebody who doesn't respect your consent, and nobody wants to be in arelationship like that.

  • Not providing emotional support. BDSM can be very emotionally and psychologically draining. A good partner will understand this, and provide comfort and support when it's needed, including after scenes. You should never be made to feel bad for being emotional, or for needing comfort.

  • Using punishment inconsistently, or out of scene. Punishment is, for a lot of people, part of BDSM. But punishments should always be something which reflects the submissive's behaviour, not the Dominant's mood. If your Dominant punishes the same behaviour one day and not the next, that is a warning sign. If your Dominant ever punishes you without being in scene (that is, without you having specifically agreed that you are in a BDSM dynamic at the time), that is abuse.

  • Putting you in risky situations. Of course, there's an element of risk in any BDSM. But it can be minimised (see our “Safe BDSM Practice” tab for more on how to do this), and if your Dominant is putting you in any situation which is likely to cause you serious physical or emotional damage, this is unaccaptable. Similarly, if you express a concern about safety, your Dominant should take it seriously and take the time to put your mind at rest. A partner who puts your health and wellbeing at risk is not a partner you should trust.

  • Telling you something is okay “because I'm a Dominant”. While, yes, some Dominant behaviours are things that would be considered abuse without context, those behaviours still require consent. Indeed, it is your consent, not their Dominance that stops them being abuse. If your Dominant is doing something to you, and you tell them you're not comfortable with it, they should stop – no matter the situation, no matter the action.

  • Telling you that you have to trust them. Trust is earned. It is entirely normal and okay not to trust somebody entirely, and while they may be hurt to know that you don't trust them, they should also understand. If somebody is making you feel guilty for not trusting them... well, honestly, that's the best sign there is that you were right not to.

  • Using guilt as a weapon. You should never be intentionally made to feel guilty by your partner. Whether this takes the form of making it all about them, bringing up an unrelated slight, or telling you that you're “doing kink wrong”, this is abusive behaviour. If you spend a lot of time in the relationship feeling guilty or ashamed, it may be time to consider that the problem is with them, not you.

If you see any of these behaviours in your relationship, or if you feel threatened or abused by your partner in any way, the best thing to do is to discuss it with your partner, or to get out of the relationship. Never let anyone make you feel like a bad person for leaving a relationship. You have the right to be happy and healthy, in your love life as much as any other aspect, and you have no responsibility to stay in a relationship which makes you miserable. Even if you don't think your partner is being intentionally abusive, a relationship can still be toxic, and you should be absolutely free to leave it if you feel like that's what's best for you.

This list of danger signs is skewed towards the assumption that the reader is a submissive, but it's important to know that Dominants can also be abused by their partners, and have just as much right to leave a relationship which is causing them harm. If you are a Dominant and you are uncomfortable with something in your relationship, your submissive should take this just as seriously as if it is the other way around.

If you don't feel that it's safe to talk to your partner about your concerns, or to leave the relationship, then help is available. You can find information on this in our “Useful Resources” tab, or by looking into local shelters and help groups. Don't be afraid to tell the authorities. While it may be embarrassing to talk about being in a BDSM relationship, the truth is that it isn't that unusual. Don't be afraid of being judged, or of shocking the authorities. It's better to have a short, potentially awkward conversation than to spend any longer in a toxic relationship. This applies no matter what your relationship to your partner was, if you had one, before kink became a part of it.

Whatever your role or level of kink, you deserve a happy, healthy relationship with someone who respects and cares for you.
